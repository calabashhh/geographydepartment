GES 411 Scope and Philosophy

Assignment 2

Alex Broz

Oct. 11, 2023

The history and trajectory of the study of Geography is fraught and full of anxieties. Though this history is not necessarily tied to what happened to the department at Harvard, it is still a quite useful place from which to focus our lens. When we read the history of the Geography Department at Harvard we see a few things at play (Smith, 1987). First there are the characters, representatives of their classes and circumstances acting in roles in which we are not entirely surprised by. Second, there are the textured themes of the institutional Academy at the middle of the twentieth century. Third, there is the particular intellectual history of the field of Geography up until this inflection point, and then what has transpired since then.

The further in time from the viewer an event is will change the perspective and the understanding. Too close too it; and it is too massive to comprehend. Like sitting in the front row of a movie theater. While the immediacy of the event tends to produce more emotion, and visceral connection; generally one must be a certain distance away from it in time to gain a sense of what has transpired following the event. Looking back towards the inception of the study of Geography in the mid 1870s from our vantage point in 2023, we look over a chasm of about 150 years and find that the elimination of Geography at Harvard take places almost directly between these two points. A symmetry that maybe we can use to help us better understand things.

Why did Geography generally emerge out of the study of Geology in the US University systems? If we can cast ourselves back to that time, we will understand that Industrial Capital is expanding its spread further across the Globe. In the United States following the US Civil War, where Northern Industrial Capital dominated the Southern Slave based Agricultural Capital, a newly empowered national Industrial Capital began to emerge. This churning machine needs resources and people and land to feed its ravenous hunger. The study of Geology at these educational institutions allows for this capital to better find and extract the mineral wealth it requires. It is employed as a tool for imperial industrial capital. This is the environment which Geography emerges out of Geography.

While these both are tools for studying the earth and human's relation to it and the other people and things upon it, the Academy is a participant in this project. Producing the competent professional managerial class it requires to out compete the other voracious national amalgams of Capital and Power. Geographers have for long before the advent of the early modern capital driven world been involved in the work of imperial colonial projects. Long before our globe encircling satellite network, there was a time when people did not necessarily know the shape of continents or the origins of rivers. Geographers used their knowledge to map and understand the regions of the world, and were encouraged to do so by political power in order to capitalize upon this information about where advantage lay. Trade routes, the locations of desert oasis, and gold deposits all these were deeply guarded secrets.

This knowledge of the landscape of the outside world has always been entangled with the expansion of political power. So it is no surprise to see it still being entangled with it as political power becomes infused with the emergent national amalgams of modern industrial capital in the late 19th century.

This strain of geographic thought and practice as it came into its own in the Academy sought the legitimacy of other departments and fields. As legitimacy in this modern context was primarily seen as scientific and rational so to must Geography strive to achieve these goals in its own realm. Here we see environmental determinism, the mapping of racial characteristics, and other pseudo-science endeavors at the vanguard of geographical scholarship and thought. Geographers were doing the work of empire here. Mackinder (1904) for example while seeming to outline some truism about the centrality of Eurasia was instead merely making a justification for Britain's own massive empire and continued expansion. With the fiery conflagration which started in 1914 and ended with the Allied victory in 1945, this search for a unified approach to geographic thought fell out of favor. The pseudo-science was discredited and so with it went most of the field's search for a unifying theme. Even the notion of having a unified theme seemed suspect.

This is the larger Academy background into which we find the cancellation of Harvard's Geography department following the concluding of the world wars. It is notable that in early the years of the 20th century the study of geography in the US became much more focused providing military assistance. This makes some sense as a continuation of the imperial project which it participated in in the late 19th century. With the closing of the frontier, the great national based amalgams of industrial capital no longer were contending for territory which was unspoken for, so to speak.

It is interesting that Derwent Whittlesey had assumed that there would be a great out flowing of funds from the victorious US government towards Geography programs in the US, like Harvard's following the end of the two world wars and in the service of establishing a post-war Pax Americana. This piece of information seems to support the notion that so much of the push behind Geographical study in the US Academy was in the support of imperial projects. Why this did not materialize is still worth studying. Perhaps it is due to the nature of this new US Capital dominant hegemony. Perhaps it felt that the kind of expertise which geographers could provide was not useful or compatible with the type of domination it was producing. Or it could be that focus for the development of producing new imperial tools lay outside the usually dominant centers of established power in the North East of the US, and instead was being spent in the newly emergent West and Sunbelt areas. This direction and interruption of resources from the center seems to play a much larger role in the future and direction of Geography at Harvard than the proximate causes of homophobia and inter-departmental politics. While these certainly played major roles in the events of the discontinuation of the department, I do not believe that they are or were unique to Harvard or to this time period. They might offer an immediate explanation to understand this particular case, but they are not sufficient in and of themselves to explain the predicament of Geography as a whole.

This piece takes a similar view of the circumstances surrounding the downfall of Geography departments at ANU (Lahiri-Dutt, 2018) and at the University of Michigan (Huntley & Rosenblum, 2020). While there might be proximate causes related to misogyny, racism, the loss of significant figures within the department, or political disagreements with the faculty; none of these are especially unique to Geography departments and none of them are able to explain the "existential anxieties" within Geography.

Harvard's discontinuation of Geography is to be taken as a symbol, an inflection point in the history of the field of Geography, after which the flood gates of retrenchment seemed to open. As a field, it continues to struggle intellectually to define itself. With the emergence of a Geography separate from Geology, Geography found itself with two parts: one wedded to the physical, the geomorphic; and the other attempting to describe a human connection. This split identity was able to find intellectual purchase with the pursuit of "truth" through the exploration of the idea environmental determinism. With this idea being discredited, there was no unified idea to bring its parts together. Though theorists within the field have made attempts to bridge this gap it seems clear through the intervening years and through the cases at both U of M and ANU that the discipline still resides in this no-mans-land, wandering the wilderness in the aftermath.

Geographers have even given this term a name and discuss it frequently, the "unity myth". This idea, which if Geographers are unable to agree upon one object of their study, then Geography must instead be the invisible bridge which connects all these disparate strains of hard and social sciences. However, it seems clear that this unity myth is mostly just that. This myth is a shibboleth for Geographers who are unable to agree upon what they all do together. Taking the example of U of M and in looking at their list of professors and their areas of study it is notable that though many will speak about Geography being a bridge, the faculty was pretty clearly divided between two halves and that there was no obvious crossover between cultural and physical.

This intellectual drift has of course been exacerbated by the neo-liberal turn of the last quarter or so of the 20th century assaulting the Academia, which looks for ways to extract value and cut funding in the service of supposed "efficiency". This pressure has has bared down across the Academy and caused much consternation and tribulation. The competition for funding, for students; between institutions, departments, and faculty members has only worked to further degrade and demoralize. The largest and most egregious take away from the study of the naming changes of various Geography and Geography related departments in the US and Canada was how vapid it all is. Academics trying to ape the workings of marketers. For faculty to all but ask students to tell them what their department or class should be called only fits further into both this neo-liberal turn as well as the decades long drift for the field.

If the field still is unable to come up with a convincing explanation for what the main object of their inquiry is which can encompass all that Geographers do, then it certainly seems unlikely that it can do so under these current trends which see the costs of higher education rising with no end in sight and thus ever greater pressures which will be brought to bear upon the field. I cannot see how this field can hold together without a unifying object of purpose. Of course the current trends within the Academy also seem unlikely that a unifying direction will come about. It does seem however that Geographers in the Academy are quite fearful of this loss of departmental status. Though it is unclear if this fear around this stems from the loss of power, prestige, and job security that are centered within a department or if it has anything to do with needing a department to give validity and intellectual gravity to their teaching and research.

We certainly have seen how the arc of US geography departments have developed out of the contingency of circumstance and necessity rising out of Geology in the mid 19th century. The status of a department is cherished, but is it needed? Does it best serve the study of Geography? Clearly Geography is nothing if not an interdisciplinary subject and it is this interdisciplinarity which is both its strength and weakness. While some academics may dream of an interdisciplinary higher education with fewer or even no traditional departments, it generally remains only that, a dream. And under the current circumstances of economics and politics it is unlikely that it will be a strong trend reshaping higher ed anytime soon. It does seem to be however the best option and the one most suited to the nature of Geography. Geography is the study of space and place, it is an attempt to understand the relations between things within space and the perspective we have on those things and how positions within space affect those perspectives. Geography is concerned with things at a range of scales: there is a global geography, national geography, regional geography, local geography, a domestic geography, and even a microscopic geography. It is this nature which causes it to be so challenged by the current limited configuration of departments within a US Academic context.

Perhaps this is to be expected. It is quite curious that Herodotus is both the father of Geography as well as History. He certainly did not set out to create two fields of study or inquiry, but I do not think it is an accident that these two fields have a united origin. Both are the study of quite elemental features of existence: space and time. Both of these elements are, as we have come to understand through contemporary physics, neither as simple or as separate from each other as was commonly thought. Of course, though we may understand through the theoretical lens of physics these truths, it is still something that we mere humans have difficulty understanding or experiencing in ways which are universally understood and expressed. It is the human-centered-ness of these phenomena which we cannot escape and yet it is this which we must use to understand ourselves and our world.

While much hay has been made about Geography being a bridge between the hard and the social sciences. We also know that such distinctions are themselves arbitrary. But we do also know that it has continually come up short in this supposed role between the two. And yet maybe, it still could be that bridge, but perhaps the issue is less that it is too ambitious than it is not ambitious enough. Why cannot Geography, like History, embrace its humanity and seek to be a bridge between, not just the hard and social sciences but the humanities as well? This is not a likely path for the field, and certainly not a lucrative one, but it seems to offer more than can the cul-de-sac it finds itself in.
